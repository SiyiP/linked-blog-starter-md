POCUS - different from formal US - a doctor does it to answer a specific dx question, in real time at bedside to guide the plan and assessment. 

can help detect acute life threatening conditions , inexpensive with no ionizing radiation or contrast. portable. 
- use of sound waves -> reflecting back to transducer ... depends on hitting the tissue making the image. 

isoechoic = same , gray , as surrounding tissues. 
hyperechoic - white, brighter, muscles/diaphragm
anechoic - black fluid, absence of echos, any form of liquids = blood vessels, cysts and ascites. 
hypoechoic - less black than surrounding structures - a bit darker like blackish but not full black. = solid organs 

types of transducers
- linear = high freq, 6 cm, the probe face is linear. high freq = high resolution, but low depth... **note good for vascular , goood for superficial structures, breast, thyroid, skin/soft tissue, joints, bones**
- curvilinear - deeper 30 cm , low frequency - for **abdomen, pelvis, lumbar spine, pleural effusion.** - commmonly used for peritoneum and detect fluid
- phased array - smaller face, little square, 35 cm, specifically for heart and lungs bc.**the small face can fit bt the intercostal spaces to visualize heart and lungs deep to ribs.**

longitudinal plane - long axis - hot dog style with height , **probe marker towards their head**
transverse plane / short axis - **probe marker to patient right side.**

lung exam protocol - can get good with repetition , 90% accuracy and for acutely dyspneic patients. detection of 100+ mL and <20 mL with 100% sensitivity = **better than CXR and CT in many cases for the lung!!!**
modified BLUE protocol 
- start with the probe, and we have a 3 point anatomic exam per hemithorax - look at 3 locations
- anterior = mid-clavicular, 2nd intercostal to look at upper lobes
- lat zone - middle/lingular lobes near the midaxillary line, 4/5th intercostal space
- posterior zone - lower lobes, rarely can measure bc pt can't sit up, but this is looking where lung connects to diaphragm 
	this is PLAPS point = most posterior point. **posterolat anterior pulm syndrome - high yield for any pleural effusions/alveolar consolidations.** = most of your pathology is here! 
these exams = lung POCUS = always in **longitudinal plane, going to the 3 zones.**
- linear probe = visualize pleural line. 
	- pleural line = **very first bright hyperechoic line you see after skin, soft tissue + rib**
	- ribs are also bright, but there's shadowing under it specifically 
- phased array probe - for lung parenchyma, lung itself. 

algorithm lung PCUS 
1. lung sliding? - visceral pleura will slide freely with respirations.rules out pneumothorax meaning slide = air can't be there. 
	1. pneumothorax specifically is ruled in by lung point = 100% specific = **we seee some shimmering/movement of the pleural line, but one spot where it stops, a transition pt bt aerated lung + air in pleural space!!! where air enters.**
2. A-lines? - horizontal lines deep ot pleural line, **reverberations of pleural line,** when echo waves hit air, they scatter, which means it's an artifact meaning **air is present below the pleural line!!**... 
3. B-lines? - hyperechoic, vertical lines... **originate from pleural line and move with lung sliding, all the way downward to periphery**... also an artifact, meaning septa of alveoli(usually so microscopic that it's not seen... is getting super thick!!!)
	1. basically more than 3 B lines = pahtologic in a single intercostal space = **thickening fo interlobular septa, fluid, pulm edema, ARDS, infection, fibrosis etc.**
4. consolidation?- more obvious to see - lack of air in lungs, **looks like liver, hepatcization, no anechoic, but more isoechnoic, and hyperechoic areas which move with respiration, air bronchograms/trapped aerated spaces that move**
	1. shred sign = see a bunch of B lines. 
5. effusion? - **anechoic** space surrounded by typical anatomic boundaries, **best seen at PLAPS point**
	1. jellyfish sign - tip of the lung which will float in space, effusion so large. consolidated lung caused by effusion. 
	2. fibrous septations seen = lights criteria, this is 
	3. exudative effusions - tons of proteins ofc = causing **fibrins to connect and cause septations.**

FAST exam - eFAST? 
- in the ER and trauma all the time 
- extended focused asssessment w sonography in trauma, adjunct to the ABCs. 
for assessing = intra-abd hemorrhage + hemothorax in RUQ/LUQ and pelvic windows
hemopericardium = subocstal windows
pneumothorax = anterior lung windows 

but there's low sensitivity for retroperitoneal hemorrhage can't visualize the retroperitoneal space well. 

with intra abd hemorrhage = **curvilinear or phased array probe.**
- binary - is there fluid/blood? yes or no? 
- RUQ - longitudinal plane , midaxillary line 
	- supradiaphragmatic space, PLAPs pt 
	- subdiaphragmatic space 
	- morison's pouch - hepatorenal space , between liver + R kidney 
		- **most sensitive spot to see free fluid in a supine patient for 1 hour.**
	- inferior pole of kidney 
- LUQ - longitudinal plane midaxillary line 
	- - supradiaphragmatic space, PLAPs pt 
	- subdiaphragmatic space 
	- splenorenal space 
	- inferior pole of kidney 
	- note - due to the splenocolic ligament blocking the splenorenal space, we will see free fluid fill above the diaphragm before the spleenorenal space. 
- Pelvis = scan transverse AND longitudinal. 
	- rectouterine pouch/pouch of douglas in women. 
	- rectovesicular space in men. 

hemopericardium - subcostal window 
- cardiac - one instance where **probe marker to LEFT side., in transverse plane.**
- probe held overhead , rotated down and UP, fan it up to flashlight the beam into the chest right under xiphoid process. 
see fluid in the heart = to OR for exploratory lap, unstable pt with poz FAST exam. 
unstable pt with negative FAST = consider retroperitoneal hemorrhage, external hemorrhage or do serial eFASTs. 
chronic liver failure + no acute symptoms and see free fluid in the hemopericardium = paracentesis for cirrhosis. monitor stability + urgent CT/echo

cardio
- use a cardiac POCUS phased array rpobe for small view 
- note that this is qualitative assessment of LV systolic function, volume, etc. but not replace formal Echo!!!
5 views
- parasternal long axis = PLAX = **ideally the AV and MV are on the same plane, seen on the same screen.** 
- parasternal short axis - PSAX = view of the IV septum, the papillary muscles in the circle of the LV. a slide of the heart like a hamburger. 
- apical 4-chamber = A4C = at PMI, apex of heart. we'll have to point it towards the heart , by fanning. see mitral and tricuspid valve, all 4 chambers. 
	- note - the **marker will be put to the Left side, probe over LV apex.**
- subcostal 4 chamber 
	- probe to the LEFT side, scooping under the xiphoid 
- IVC - seeing where it enters into the RA... probe to patient head and see the liver, hepatic vein dumping into the IVC. at the most anterior IVC... at the true diameter of the IVC, seeing it is crucial to get the diameter . 

CV access
- put it in the Internal jugular vein 
- most superficial = closest to probe face, SCM, then deep = internal juvugular vein, then posterior to that (more internal) is the carotid artery, then posterior to that is trachea.

vascular pocus - for venous access, peripheral IVs. 
- for vascular pocus - linear probe is perfect,high resolution, low depth. 
- understand v vs artery vs nerve/lymph nodes
	-- arteries - light pressure noncompressible. high pressure vessel, htick echogenic walls. pulsatile.
	veins = low pressure, compress ez, thin/imperceptible walls, non-pulsatile. continuous color doppler. 

color doppler - 
- blue = blood flows away from transducer
- red = blood towards transducer
- brighter = fast 
- dull = slower 

nerves = look like veins but they are hyperechoic... 
lymph nodes = tilt the transducer to identify the node margins. difference is to see the walls of lymph node go away.
