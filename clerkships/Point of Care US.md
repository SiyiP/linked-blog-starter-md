POCUS - different from formal US - a doctor does it to answer a specific dx question, in real time at bedside to guide the plan and assessment. 

can help detect acute life threatening conditions , inexpensive with no ionizing radiation or contrast. portable. 
- use of sound waves -> reflecting back to transducer ... depends on hitting the tissue making the image. 

isoechoic = same , gray , as surrounding tissues. 
hyperechoic - white, brighter, muscles/diaphragm
anechoic - black fluid, absence of echos, any form of liquids = blood vessels, cysts and ascites. 
hypoechoic - less black than surrounding structures - a bit darker like blackish but not full black. = solid organs 

types of transducers
- linear = high freq, 6 cm, the probe face is linear. high freq = high resolution, but low depth... **note good for vascular , goood for superficial structures, breast, thyroid, skin/soft tissue, joints, bones**
- curvilinear - deeper 30 cm , low frequency - for **abdomen, pelvis, lumbar spine, pleural effusion.** - commmonly used for peritoneum and detect fluid
- phased array - smaller face, little square, 35 cm, specifically for heart and lungs bc.**the small face can fit bt the intercostal spaces to visualize heart and lungs deep to ribs.**

lung exam pprotocol - can get good with repetition 
modified BLUE protocol 
- start with the probe, and we have a 3 point anatomic exam per hemithorax - look at 3 locations
- anterior = midclavicular, 2nd intercostal to look at upper lobes
- lat zone - middle/lingular lobes near the midaxillary line, 4/5th intercostal space
- posterior zone - lower lobes, rarely can measure bc pt can't sit up, but this is looking where lung connects to diaphragm 
	this is PLAPS point = most posterior point. **posterolat anterior pulm syndrome - high yield for any pleural effusions/alveolar consolidations.** = most of your pathology is here! 
these exams = lung POCUS = always in **longitudinal p**
